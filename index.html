Reinforcement Learning · Generalization Study

Does a Snake Agent
Learn, or Memorize?

Comparing DQN, DoubleDQN, and PPO across spatial scaling, obstacle introduction, and feature representation in the Snake game environment.

DQN Double DQN PPO 9×9 → 6×6–12×12 Hamiltonian Normalization Distribution Shift
Section I

Introduction

Reinforcement learning agents are almost always evaluated in the same environment where they were trained. This creates a challenge whereby we treat high training reward as evidence of true learning, when it may only reflect memorized environment-specific patterns. An agent that achieves high scores on a 9×9 Snake grid might be exploiting certain spatial regularities of that exact grid, those that disappear the moment the board grows by three squares or a single obstacle appears.

The Snake game is a clear example of this phenomenon as it reveals some generalization challenges inherent in reinforcement learning. The structure of the task is simple enough to learn and perform well, yet a small change, such as enlarging the grid or placing a wall, can fundamentally alter which states the agent encounters and what strategies are rewarded. This makes the game an ideal testing arena for studying distribution shifts, i.e. the gap between what an agent learned during training and what it needs to do at test time.

Our central question is whether a DQN trained on a 9×9 Snake grid learns a transferable policy, or whether it memorises environment-specific trajectories that fail under distribution shift. To address this, we compare DQN, DoubleDQN, and PPO across spatial scaling (6×6 → 12×12 grids), obstacle introduction, and feature representation (local, extended, and hybrid state encodings). To interpret generalisation performance, we need to be considerate with metric selection, because raw food/step can be confounded by grid geometry. This motivates our use of Hamiltonian-normalised performance as the primary measure. Under this metric, we find that all three agents generalise more effectively to larger grids than smaller ones, consistent with our hypothesis that state expansion preserves the structure of the training environment in a way that state shrinkage does not.

Section II

Theoretical Background

Deep Reinforcement Learning in Snake

Previous work on the Snake environment relied on reinforcement learning and Deep Q-Network to train models and evaluate. Almalki and Wocjan (2019) train precisely using the DQN model and SARSA (state-action-reward-state-action) algorithm for the same game. Their work reveals that this framework provides better learning results compared to previous techniques used for similar simulations. A more recent work by Kommalapati et al. (2026) confirms the model's outstanding performance in Snake environments. Our project builds on this scientific literature, where we adopt their agent training framework for our game. But we go further to explore model generalization across modified environments.

The Generalization Problem in RL

In supervised learning, generalization is explicitly measured by first training the models on one dataset and evaluating on a held-out test set. Reinforcement learning, however, lacks this clean separation. Cobbe et al. (2019) demonstrates this in CoinRun, where agents overfit to surprisingly large training sets, achieving near-perfect performance on familiar levels while failing on structurally identical but visually new ones. The agents needed thousands of procedurally generated levels before they could generalize.

This is analogous to a student who memorizes past exams. They would be able to pass every test they have seen, but will fail when the questions are rephrased.

Similarly, as the distribution shifts such failure is expected from reinforcement learning models more formally. When an agent trained on MDP M_train is evaluated on M_test, the state distribution it encounters at test time differs from training. The Q-values learned during training are defined over the training distribution, and they may be arbitrarily wrong on states that were rarely or never visited during training. For Snake, moving from 9×9 to 12×12 changes the density of the grid, the average distance to food, and the likelihood of any given state pattern. Theoretically, the agent's Q-function has no meaningful predictions for these new states.

Curriculum Learning and Training Diversity

One response to the generalization problem is curriculum learning, where training environments are structured to become gradually harder as the agent encounters them, thus, building transferable skills (Narvekar et al., 2020). This approach underlines some of the most celebrated RL successes which include self-play curricula in AlphaGo and league based training methods against increasingly capable opponents in AlphaStar. The core idea is that exposure to varied training conditions is necessary for generalization. In our experiment, we test whether this curriculum engineering is even necessary; if standard algorithms trained on a single environment can generalise sufficiently, the added complexity of curriculum design may not be warranted.

Cobbe et al. (2019) show in the Procgen benchmark how training with variety, sometimes compared to Unsupervised Environment Design (UED), dramatically narrows the gap between train and test performance, but only when agents see enough diverse levels (hundreds to thousands). Our experiments suggest that at least for Snake, this is not really necessary: our agents trained exclusively on a 9×9 grid, with no environmental variety whatsoever, transfer reasonably well to larger environments, suggesting that for upward spatial scaling at least, diversity of training conditions may be less critical due to the relative simplicity of Snake.

Section III

Experimental Design

We implement a custom Snake environment with configurable grid size (6–12), obstacle count (0–8), and state representation. The snake starts at the center, food is placed randomly at each episode, and movement is deterministic. Three representation schemes encode the agent's view:

  • Local (11 features): Immediate danger in three directions, food direction (4 bits), current heading (4 bits). Scale-invariant by design.
  • Extended (19 features): Local features plus 8 raycasts (distance to nearest wall or body segment in each direction, normalized by grid size).
  • Hybrid (26 features): Extended features plus global context including body fraction, obstacle density, Manhattan distance to food, and quadrant occupancy distribution.

All agents are trained exclusively on a 9×9 grid with zero obstacles, then evaluated across the full grid size and obstacle sweep. Three algorithms are compared: DQN (150k steps, buffer size 100k, ε-greedy), DoubleDQN (same, with decoupled selection/evaluation to reduce overestimation bias), and PPO (800k steps, 16 parallel environments, GAE). Training uses 3 independent random seeds per configuration; results show mean ± CI across seeds.

Training the Snake Agent

a. The Setup

First, it is important for us to understand what we are doing when we train the DQN model. All DQN and DoubleDQN agents are trained on a fixed 9×9 Snake grid with no obstacles, using 8 parallel environments running simultaneously. Each agent trains for 150,000 environment steps, which is roughly 18,000 gradient updates before being frozen and evaluated on a battery of held-out configurations. The PPO agents run longer at 800,000 steps, using 16 parallel environments, to account for the higher per-step compute cost of on-policy learning.

The evaluation metric throughout is food collected per step, which is a scale-invariant measure that lets us compare performance across grids of different sizes without the raw score being confounded by episode length. It is important to note however that this evaluates efficiency, not episodic performance, but we use it because we are interested in comparing the generalisation performance down the line. Below we show some of the hyperparameters we experimented with when creating our final model.

  • Learning Rate — 5e-4 Controls how large a gradient step Adam takes per update. We lowered this from 1e-3 in v2 after observing that higher learning rates create greater instability. The tradeoff for such a low learning rate is convergence speed, but at 150k steps we nevertheless observe a plateau in performance as the model converges towards its optimal policy.
  • Epsilon Decay — 0.9998 → 0.05 We utilise a very slow multiplicative decay here. However, after repeated trials we realised that this only controls when the agent converges, not what score it converges to. Indeed, halving or doubling the decay rate produced agents that hit the same final plateau earlier or later but landed in the same place. This applies for both linear and exponential decay formulations. We speculate that this is because Snake's state space is compact enough that even short exploration phases cover the meaningful state-action pairs. Hence the slow decay is more of a stability measure rather than a hyperparameter that meaningfully alters the results.
  • Replay Buffer — 100,000. We increased this from a previous version at 50,000. In general, larger buffers decorrelate samples better because each mini-batch pulls from a wider spread of the training history. However, there is the issue of staleness: early transitions collected by a near-random policy keep getting mixed into batches. For our purposes, we find that given Snake's small state space, the staleness cost is low because even old transitions remain structurally informative. Hence, the diversity benefit dominates.
  • Discount Factor — 0.99. Arguably, this is the most important parameter. In general, food is sparse relative to step count: on a 9×9 grid with up to 243 steps per episode, an agent might eat only a handful of times. With γ = 0.99, a reward 100 steps away still carries 37% of its face value; at γ = 0.95 it would be under 1%. We hence made sure that discount factor was relatively close to 1 to make sure that the agent didn't just chase the distance directing shaped reward and ignore long-horizon food collection.
  • Grad Clip — 5.0. This prevents the optimizer from taking overly large steps when the TD error of the agent spikes, for example on the agent's first food collection or death event. We changed our value from 10 to 5 to match our lower learning rate: both parameters work together to keep updates conservative.

c. Other changes and experiments

Aside from the parameter changes, there were some additional things that we experimented with before settling on our final model. The first attempt to reduce variance in v2 was adding Prioritized Experience Replay (PER), which means that we sampled transitions with high TD error more frequently, on the theory that the agent would focus on its most informative mistakes. The implementation also added an immediate single-step learn_single update at every env step, in addition to the batched PER update. Unfortunately, this did not work; the learn_single update was unweighted (treating all transitions as equally important), while PER used importance-sampling weights to correct for the biased sampling. Running both simultaneously meant the network was receiving corrected and uncorrected gradient signals at every step, which effectively undid PER's bias correction in the same pass. As a result, performance dropped to near-random. We removed PER entirely and went back to uniform replay.

PPO Training

Where DQN stores and replays past experience, PPO collects a fresh batch of transitions, uses them, and throws them away. This means that the hyperparameter calculations have to be very different.

a. The Setup

PPO agents train for 800,000 environment steps across 16 parallel environments, producing roughly 50 policy updates over the course of training. Each update collects a rollout of 1,024 steps per environment (16,384 transitions per batch) before running 10 epochs of mini-batch gradient descent over that data. After those 10 epochs, the rollout is discarded and the process repeats.

The 800k step budget is not directly comparable to DQN's 150k. Because PPO can't reuse transitions, raw step counts measure something different: DQN at 150k steps has seen perhaps 2–3 million gradient updates worth of signal via replay; PPO at 800k has seen exactly 800k transitions, each used at most 10 times before being discarded. Below we show some of the hyperparameters we experimented with when creating our final model.

b. The PPO Hyperparameters

Learning Rate1e-4
PPO Epochs10
Reward NormalizationTrue
  • Learning Rate — 1e-4. This is lower than DQN's 5e-4, which reflects a structural difference between the two algorithms. DQN updates a value function toward a fixed target; PPO updates a policy and value function simultaneously, and the policy update is already constrained by the clipping mechanism. Pushing a larger learning rate against that constraint tends to cause the policy to oscillate around the clip boundary rather than converge through it. Our experimentation showed that a rate of about 1e-4 keeps the optimization conservative without stalling progress.
  • PPO Epochs — 10. Number of passes over the collected rollout before it's discarded. More epochs squeeze more gradient signal out of each batch, improving sample efficiency. However, we observed that if we trained for too long after convergence the model performance tended to deteriorate.
  • Reward Normalization — True. This matters because Snake's reward signal is unbalanced: most steps return a small negative shaping signal (−0.05 per step, +/− distance reduction), but food and death events create large outlier rewards (+10 and −10). Normalizing flattens the distribution so the network learns from the full reward structure rather than just the extremes.

c. Remaining Challenges

For both DQN and PPO, we were only able to run three seeds, which increased our variance and increased the spread of our confidence intervals. In our case, the practical constraint was compute: on a T4, three seeds at the current step budget already takes around six hours. More seeds means a proportionally longer run, which becomes impractical for an iterative research loop.

PPO trains for 800,000 env steps while DQN trains for 150,000. This is not a controlled comparison since PPO gets more than five times the experience. The asymmetry exists because PPO's on-policy nature means it can't reuse data the way DQN can, so raw step counts aren't directly comparable. But it means any advantage PPO shows could partly reflect the larger training budget rather than algorithmic superiority. Matching on wall-clock time rather than env steps would be a cleaner experimental design, though harder to implement.

Section IV

Baselines

Random Agent

At each step, the random agent selects uniformly among the three relative actions (straight, left, right). It is entirely ignorant of food position or collision risk, making it a true lower bound. In practice, random agents frequently survive for surprisingly many steps on open grids since random walks avoid walls passably, but their food-per-step rate is very low because food encounters are accidental. This makes the random baseline especially useful for detecting whether an agent is doing anything beyond "not dying."

Hamiltonian Cycle Baseline

The Hamiltonian baseline follows a pre-computed cycle that visits every cell of the grid exactly once before returning to start. Because it never misses a cell, it is guaranteed to collect every piece of food that appears and achieves perfect food coverage (Weinberger et al., 2024). This is the theoretical ceiling for food collection, and normalizing agent performance against it gives a scale-invariant efficiency measure.

Importantly, the Hamiltonian baseline is quite slow. On an 8×8 grid it takes exactly 64 steps per food item (the full cycle length), since food can appear anywhere in the path. On larger grids, it takes correspondingly more steps. This means that raw Hamiltonian food/step rate decreases with grid size since the baseline actually becomes worse on larger grids by this metric, even though its policy is optimal in a coverage sense. As we show, this creates a systematic artifact in normalized comparisons.

Section VI

Results: Training Dynamics

Training curve visualizations reveal two fascinating trends. Firstly, in Figure 1 below, DQN and double DQN display comparable performance, suggesting that the change in model here does not make a massive difference. Secondly, PPO takes longer to train and when trained converges to a lower food/episode score than the QN policies. This suggests that there is something about Snake that is more conducive to Q learning. As has been covered in other research, (Da Rosa Filho et al) DQN is far superior to PPO in such situations for a number of reasons outlined in the next paragraph.

Fig 1. Training learning curves for DQN, DoubleDQN, and PPO on a 9×9 grid (hybrid features). Smoothed curves (solid) overlay raw episode rewards (shaded bands show seed variance). DQN and DoubleDQN converge rapidly around episode 3,000–4,000 before plateauing; PPO converges more slowly but more stably, reaching a lower asymptote by episode 6,500. The high raw variance in DQN reflects the stochasticity of ε-greedy exploration combined with the sparse food reward structure.

The first prevalence of DQN is Sample Efficiency. Q-learning is an off-policy algorithm, which uses an "experience replay" buffer to learn from past experiences multiple times. Since Snake involves repetitive movements, this ability to reuse data allows Q-learning to converge much faster than on-policy methods like PPO, which discard data after updates. Another reason for the observed difference is Discrete Action Space of the Snake environment: Up, Down, Left, Right. This is an optimal environment for DQN, whereas PPO is much better at continuous action states. Last but not least, the Reward Structure of the game further contributes to favoring the DQN algorithm. In Snake, rewards are provided step by step (dying or eating food). Q-learning is highly effective at propagating these immediate rewards back through the state space to build an optimal policy.

Section VII

In-Distribution Benchmark (9×9, No Obstacles)

Interestingly, however, when we change our metric to one of efficiency (food/step within an episode instead of food/episode), the PPO does better compared to DQN. Yet, it is important to note that this difference is relatively small and is within our overall margin of error. This implies to us that PPO learns policies that are similarly efficient compared to DQN but its long run performance when the snake gets longer is less comparable, implying that there is a difference and maybe even a tradeoff between efficient and long lasting policies in Snake.

This is probably because snake is simple enough such that a small dimensional representation of the feature space is sufficient to allow our agent to learn good policies.

Section VIII

Size Generalization

We now turn to the question of generalisation, evaluating models trained on a 9×9 grid across grid sizes of 6×6, 8×8, 10×10, and 12×12. Examining the raw Food/Step in Figure 4 below, performance appears to degrade monotonically as grid size increases. However, this is misleading: Food/Step is mechanically sensitive to grid geometry, as a larger grid requires the agent to travel further between food items on average, reducing the metric regardless of policy quality. A decline in Food/Step therefore cannot be straightforwardly interpreted as a decline in generalisation ability.

Figure 4: Generalisation performance according to grid size, food/step

To correct for this, we normalise agent performance against the Hamiltonian path collector, which serves as a geometry-aware baseline by representing the theoretical upper bound of deterministic coverage. We can observe that in figure 5 below, the performance after normalisation reveals a strikingly different picture: our agents generalise considerably better to larger grids than smaller ones. We posit that this reflects a structural property of the task; a 10×10 grid contains the 9×9 training environment as a strict subset, meaning that state expansion preserves the learnt policy's coverage. State shrinkage, by contrast, breaks this containment relationship, and the learnt policy must operate in an environment that is not a superset of its training distribution, explaining the relatively weaker performance on smaller grids.

Figure 5: Generalisation performance according to size, agent/hamiltonian

It should be noted that the Hamiltonian baseline is itself not grid-size-neutral: the Hamiltonian path becomes increasingly inefficient as grid size grows, causing the normalised ratio to trend upward in part due to a shrinking denominator rather than purely improving agent performance. Our normalised results for larger grids should therefore be interpreted conservatively, as the metric carries an upward bias that increases with grid size. Nevertheless, Hamiltonian normalisation remains a better comparison than the raw metric as it compares our model with a policy baseline.

Results are robust across seeds

We observe in Figure 6 below that as grid size increases, cross-seed variance widens with decreasing grid size across all three agents. This is possibly because small grids are inherently noisier, allowing single bad decisions or fortunate food placement to disproportionately affect the Food/Step metric between seeds. However, that being said, cross-seed variance remains relatively modest across all grid sizes, suggesting that the learnt policies are still relatively robust. Nevertheless, three seeds is a limited sample, and using more seeds in future work would provide us with greater confidence.

Section IX

Obstacle Generalization

Moving on to the second type of generalization, we focus on how the models behave with the randomized addition of obstacles. In the panel below, we can observe that, in general, the performance is decreasing as the obstacle count adds on in all. With the exception of the local feature space, in extended and hybrid feature spaces, DQN models are outperforming PPO by collecting more food per step more consistently. We utilise the random baseline here because the hamiltonian baseline does not work when obstacles are implemented, but the overall logic is the same.

Fig 7. Obstacle generalization (local × extended × hybrid)

We would like to draw special attention to the hybrid feature space in the right side of the panel. In this specific space, PPO's Food/Step drastically declines as the obstacle count increases, as opposed to the DQN models which remain more efficient and productive despite the growing challenge of each level. Moreover, the variance and error bands of DQN models are much tighter than those of PPO, suggesting a more deterministic and strategic game strategy while evaluated with 0–8 randomly placed obstacles. This is likely related to why PPO is less good at learning Snake in general, which we covered in our earlier section. Additionally, the effect being stronger the more complex the feature space is implies that the policy PPO is learning is much more overfitted to our original training space than that learned by DQN, and a higher dimensional feature space exacerbates this issue.

Additionally, unlike size generalization, obstacle generalization shows no metric reversal. In the figure below, food/step and normalized scores agree that performance degrades with more obstacles. This is because the random baseline also degrades with obstacles, so the normalization does not inflate the apparent performance of agents. The key result is that agents that were never trained with obstacles still outperform random significantly, but the gap narrows from roughly 10× at zero obstacles to approximately 5–6× at eight obstacles.

Fig 8. Obstacle generalization across models in hybrid space: food/step vs normalized to random

Ultimately, although DQN performs better relative to PPO, we can observe that there is still a drop in performance. The heatmaps below show that the difference between 9×9 training performance and test performance is positive. This suggests that in the testing environment, DQN's performance still degrades even slightly as it faces new challenges such as size variations and obstacle introductions. The size generalization on the left panel shows negative gaps at small grids, as agents perform worse on 6×6 in food/step, and positive gaps at 12×12. But as we explain, this reflects grid-size scaling effects, not genuine improvement. To the right obstacle generalization shows that the gap is consistently positive and grows with obstacle count, showing true degradation.

Section X

Discussion and Future Directions

In general, we discovered that our models (DQN, Double DQN, and PPO) generalised relatively well to different grid sizes and even when obstacles were added. We observe that when grid size is varied, agents perform better when the environment is a super set of the environment that they have been trained in, but worse when the environment is shrunk. Additionally, we observe that performance degrades as the number of obstacles increases, which is an intuitive finding that shows that the model is still not necessarily learning the optimal policy for Snake in all situations but is to some extent overfitting on the open grid model we trained it on.

One important finding we propose is regarding the methodology and selection of the metrics. As previously discussed, the direction of model generalization depends and can be easily reversed if the metric design is not considered carefully. To avoid this issue, we normalised our results with the hamiltonian baseline, as well as the random baseline in some cases. This approach helps us to achieve more reliable results and make stronger conclusions.

Future work can manipulate the model behavior with more structured obstacles and implementing more seeds. Our experiments used randomly placed obstacles, which during training can produce noisy patterns due to the absence of a learnable pattern. Using fixed corridor structures or procedurally generated mazes during training would give the agent consistent structure to learn. Finally, to strengthen the statistical rigor, later research can target 5–10 seeds, instead of 3 in our case, and observe whether the models survive multiple statistical tests.
